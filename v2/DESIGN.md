# Death Dice

## Vision & Design Philosophy (Living Game Design Document)

> Supplied by the designer 2026-06-13. This is V2's source of truth.
> Every V2 mechanic must trace back to a pillar in this document.

---

# Elevator Pitch

**Death Dice** is a chaotic multiplayer party card game where every dice roll changes the social landscape.

It combines bluffing, table politics, dramatic comebacks, deckbuilding, and explosive card interactions into fast-paced matches designed for friends hanging out in Discord.

The objective is not to create a perfectly balanced competitive card game.

The objective is to create unforgettable moments.

Players should remember ridiculous victories, hilarious betrayals, desperate last stands, impossible dice rolls, and perfectly timed reaction cards—not optimal play patterns.

If players finish a game laughing and immediately say "Again!", Death Dice has succeeded.

---

# Design Pillars

Every mechanic in Death Dice should reinforce at least one of these pillars.

## 1. Maximum Drama

Every turn should create uncertainty.

Every die roll should matter.

Every action should have the potential to completely change the direction of the game.

Players should never feel like the winner is obvious until the final moments.

The game should constantly ask:

"What crazy thing is about to happen?"

---

## 2. Social Gameplay First

Death Dice is fundamentally a social game disguised as a card game.

The best strategy should often involve reading people rather than reading spreadsheets.

Players should naturally create:

• temporary alliances

• betrayals

• revenge

• negotiation

• bluffing

• kingmaking

• table politics

The rules should encourage conversation rather than silence.

---

## 3. Controlled Chaos

Randomness exists to create stories—not frustration.

The dice should create tension without removing meaningful decisions.

Randomness should produce moments like:

"I can't believe that actually happened."

instead of:

"There was nothing I could have done."

Every random event should immediately create interesting decisions.

---

## 4. Constant Comeback Potential

No player should ever feel eliminated before they actually lose.

Being behind should unlock opportunities.

Leading should create pressure.

The leader should never feel completely safe.

The player in last place should always believe they still have a chance.

---

## 5. Fast Decisions

Players should spend their time making exciting choices.

Not calculating optimal math.

Turns should feel:

Quick.

Explosive.

Interactive.

Unexpected.

---

# Target Player Experience

Players should constantly experience:

• excitement

• uncertainty

• laughter

• celebration

• panic

• revenge

• bluffing

• cheering

• "one more game"

The game should create stories worth retelling.

# Match Length

Ideal Match

15–25 minutes

Maximum

35 minutes

Games lasting longer than this should be considered a balance issue.

---

# Design Framework

Death Dice prioritizes the following design layers.

## Micro

Low importance.

Simple controls.

Simple interactions.

No complicated execution.

Players lose because of decisions—not because of dexterity.

---

## Mezzo

Highest priority.

Everything should revolve around:

reading opponents

predicting actions

bluffing

reaction cards

social manipulation

dice tension

hidden information

politics

This is where the fun lives.

---

## Macro

Medium priority.

Enough long-term planning to reward smart players.

Never enough to require optimization spreadsheets.

The best move should rarely be obvious.

---

# Core Gameplay Loop

Every turn should follow this emotional pattern.

Roll.

React.

Adapt.

Negotiate.

Attack.

Counter.

Laugh.

Repeat.

Every phase should generate new information that changes player decisions.

---

# Dice Philosophy

The dice are the heart of Death Dice.

They are not merely a mana generator.

Every roll should create tension for the entire table.

A good roll should feel exciting.

A bad roll should still create interesting decisions.

The dice should constantly shift momentum throughout the game.

---

# Hero Philosophy

Heroes are personalities.

Not stat blocks.

Every hero should have:

A unique fantasy.

A memorable play style.

A recognizable weakness.

A signature moment.

Players should describe heroes like this:

"The gambler."

"The trickster."

"The revenge king."

"The coward."

"The wildcard."

Not:

"The one with six attack."

---

# Action Card Philosophy

Every action card should answer YES to at least one question.

Does it create drama?

Does it create interaction?

Does it create uncertainty?

Does it force difficult decisions?

Does it create memorable moments?

If the answer is no, redesign it.

---

# Party Game Metrics

Success is not measured solely by win rate.

Instead measure:

Lead changes.

Comeback victories.

Number of reaction cards played.

Average player interaction.

Political decisions.

Unexpected victories.

Clutch survival moments.

Average game length.

Table-wide events.

Turns involving three or more players.

Emotional intensity.

Story-worthy moments.

---

# The "Blue Shell" Principle

Winning should become increasingly dangerous.

Leading players naturally become larger targets.

Game systems should create pressure on leaders without making success feel unfair.

Examples include:

Bounties.

Threat mechanics.

Catch-up rewards.

Public objectives.

Risk-reward decisions.

The goal is tension—not punishment.

---

# Simulation Philosophy

The simulator exists to discover fun.

Not merely balance.

Every simulation should attempt to answer:

Was the game exciting?

Was the ending uncertain?

Were there multiple viable strategies?

Did someone come back?

Were there dramatic reversals?

Did players meaningfully interact?

Did randomness create stories?

Would real people want another game?

---

# AI Balance Framework

Simulation bots should represent different personalities.

Examples:

Aggressive

Greedy

Revenge-driven

Defensive

Chaotic

Political

Risk-taking

Self-preserving

The goal is not perfect play.

The goal is exposing emergent gameplay.

---

# Excitement Index

Death Dice should eventually compute an internal excitement score.

Possible contributors include:

Lead changes

Close finishes

Unexpected winners

Reaction card frequency

Player elimination timing

Damage swings

High-risk plays

Successful gambles

Political targeting

Comebacks

The simulator should maximize this score over time.

---

# Core Rule

Whenever there is a conflict between:

Strategic perfection

and

Party-game excitement

Choose excitement.

Always.

---

# The Ultimate Design Question

Every new mechanic should answer one simple question:

**"Will this make people yell in Discord?"**

If the answer is yes, it belongs in Death Dice.

If the answer is no, remove it.
